Introduction

This study explores patterns in artificial intelligence (AI) adoption across U.S. states using recent Census Bureau data. The primary research question examines whether there is a correlation between AI usage within key economic sectors, the dominant industry in each state, and overall state-level AI adoption. The resulting data maps the highest AI usage by state, compared with change in GDP (of the top contributing sector by state). This can be used to inform where AI adoption is most prevalent, accepted, growing, and can also be used to predict future economic changes in those areas. For the purpose of this study, the terms sector and industry are used interchangeably.

Understanding the AI Landscape

“Advances in artificial intelligence (AI) could impact nearly all aspects of society: the labor market, transportation, healthcare, education, and national security.  

AI’s effects may be profoundly positive, but the technology entails risks and disruptions that warrant attention.  

While technologists and policymakers have begun to discuss AI and applications of machine learning more frequently, public opinion has not shaped much of these conversations.  

In the U.S., public sentiments have shaped many policy debates, including those about immigration, free trade, international conflicts, and climate change mitigation. As in these other policy domains, we expect the public to become more influential over time."

(Zhang et al 2019). 

General Public Opinion (Zhang et. al. 2019):

  • More Americans support than oppose developing AI. 

  • Support is greater among those who are Male, Educated, Wealthy, or Tech Experienced.  

  • An overwhelming majority of Americans think that AI should be carefully managed (i.e. governance, data privacy, cyber attacks, surveillance, and digital manipulation).  
  • 85% of Americans report using digital products that deploy AI (e.g., navigation apps, video or music streaming apps, digital personal assistants on smartphones, etc.)

Influence of Top Sector on Short-Term GDP Growth

Choropleth Map Visualization of Top Sector Influence on GDP Using Attribute Join.  

AI Usage by Sector vs. Industry Contribution: Are industries using AI more responsible for growth? 

There is no strong positive correlation between AI usage by sector and contribution to GDP growth. The trendline is nearly flat.  

Industries like Agriculture and Construction have low AI usage but in some cases, high GDP contribution.  

States where Information is the top industry tend to have higher AI usage, but not necessarily higher GDP contribution.  

This suggests that higher AI sector usage does not directly imply stronger short-term economic growth by industry, though the long-term relationship may differ.  

Change in Real GDP by State

Change in GDP vs AI Usage : Do states with higher AI usage show more GDP growth?  

States with higher AI usage tend to show slightly better GDP performance, therefore there's a slight positive but weak relationship between AI usage and GDP change. It is important to note that correlation does not imply causation.

Conclusions

This study finds early signs of a positive but weak relationship between state-level AI usage and short-term GDP growth. However, AI adoption is still emerging, and most industries have yet to show clear, immediate economic returns from usage.  

Key takeaways include: 

  • AI uptake varies widely across industries and states. 
  • The leading industry in a state strongly influences GDP changes, but that influence is not always amplified by AI usage. Other factors such as labor, capital, local policy are also at play. 

  • While increased uptake is predicted to benefit enterprise, public opinion also demands careful governance; therefore perhaps the adoption is happening at an appropriate rate along with policy. 

  • Policy efforts should focus on enabling adoption through digital infrastructure, workforce development, and clear definitions of AI use. Targeted support may be needed in low-adoption states or sectors. 

  • Better data over time is essential. A single snapshot provides limited insight into the long-term economic impact of AI. Continued surveys and longitudinal studies will improve our understanding. 

 

Limitations

Definitions of “AI usage” vary. Some consider basic automation, others include advanced machine learning making consistency a challenge. Clearer definitions are needed.

This study compares AI usage (Q4 2023–Q1 2024) with GDP growth (Q4 2024–Q1 2025), assuming delayed economic effects. In reality, impacts may be non-linear or shaped by unrelated factors, and AI investments may take years to influence the economy.

The analysis does not account for other influences on GDP, such as natural disasters, policy changes, labor strikes, or housing markets.

This is a single snapshot in time. Longitudinal data would be needed to analyze trends or assess causality between AI use and economic growth.

State-level analysis is broad and may not show differences between tech hubs and rural areas within the same state. City-level or regional analysis could offer deeper insights.

Census data is self-reported and may include over- or underestimates.

Finally, the QGIS software is limited in its ability to conduct advanced statistical testing.
